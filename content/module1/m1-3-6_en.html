CONSUMERS AND LABEL INFORMATION

Consumers in BC appear somewhat more likely to read ingredients lists and nutrition facts labels, and those in the Atlantic Provinces are more likely to read diet and health-related claims. There also appears to be somewhat of a trend among university-educated consumers to read ingredients lists and nutrition facts tables. Those with greater than $75,000 in household income are also more likely to read nutrition facts tables than those with lower incomes.

More interestingly, consumers aged 55+ are much more likely to say they read ingredients labels, nutrition facts and diet and health-related claims to help make food purchase decisions. This is quite possibly indicative of a greater need among an aging population to be careful about its diet, especially as more consumers begin to embrace the linkage between diet and health. Women are also much more likely to participate in all of these activities than are men.

Consumers aged 55 and older and women are also more likely to feel they have control over what is in the food they buy and to say they are particular about quality and buy only the best they can afford. They are more likely to say they have thought about the way their grocery store handles food and to be the main person responsible for preparing food in the home.

Extract from the Department of Agriculture and Agri-Food Canada website – Consumer Perceptions of Food Safety and Quality – Wave 2 – Tracking 2006
