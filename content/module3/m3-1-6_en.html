Example

Listen to the example, paying close attention to the expressions used for giving an opinion.

Question:

In your opinion, is work-related stress related to the type of work a person does or to the person who occupies the position? In other words, is a surgeon more likely to be more stressed than a plumber?

Opinion:

That’s an interesting question. Personally, I would tend to say that stress is generally related to the position rather than to the person who holds the position. To me, it’s clear that the job of a surgeon is much more stressful than the job of a plumber. The lives of the surgeon’s patients depend on each decision he/she makes and there’s really no room for error, which is not the case for a plumber. I believe that some jobs which are very stressful such as surgeons, airline pilots, air traffic controllers, judges or company directors, don’t seem to be because the people who occupy these positions possess an exceptional capacity to manage their stress. In fact, if you ask me, this capacity is essential for these professions; otherwise, it would be very difficult for them to do their jobs effectively.

There’s no doubt that you could find studies that show that certain administrative assistants are more stressed than their bosses, and I’m sure that it’s possible. But in these cases, I believe that stress is not necessarily linked to the duties that the employee must accomplish, but rather to abnormal work conditions in which their duties are performed. In my opinion, we must be careful with studies that claim that workplace stress is not at all related to the job position.

In brief, I’m not saying that there aren’t exceptions, but I’m convinced that in most cases and under normal conditions, certain jobs are more demanding and more stressful than others.
