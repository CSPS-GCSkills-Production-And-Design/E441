Read the text and indicate if the statements that follow are true or false.

WORKING SAFELY AND SECURELY

Many people doubt the effectiveness of security measures in the departments and agencies of the federal government.

Increased terrorist threats around the world and the manifestation of growing violence in the workplace lead workers to question the effectiveness of the security measures in place to protect them. Do they have reason for doubt?

For certain, the federal government has taken the threats very seriously and paid special attention to security measures in the different buildings where public servants work. Therefore, when public servants enter a government building, they must prove their identity with an identity card issued by security services and carry this card with them while they are in the building.

However, one can doubt such measures as terrorist networks have become very organized. There is a question of whether the security arrangements in place will prevent them from engaging in acts of terrorism. If they can get fake passports, they can also obtain identity cards. In fact, such measures protect against petty theft or minor offenses, but not against terrorist acts.

Similarly, such measures can not counter acts of violence that are often the result of annoyances in the workplace. There is no sure way of predicting human behaviour. Apart from certain warning signs, a potentially dangerous person has no specific profile. Violent acts can be committed by an employee, a manager, a client or a family member. So, despite all the security measures in place, violence can still occur at work.

Given the uncertainty surrounding security, it is hard to believe that it is possible to operate in a workplace without any threats.

Answer choices:
According to the author, public servants must show their passports to enter government buildings.
The author believes security services must circulate during office hours.
The author thinks that there may be warning signs before an act of violence is committed.
The author’s opinion is that public servants have reason to doubt the effectiveness of security measures.
